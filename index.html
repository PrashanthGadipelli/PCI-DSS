Executive Summary

The Payment Card Industry Data Security Standard (PCI DSS v4.0.1) is the global baseline for securing cardholder data and is mandatory for every organization that stores, processes, or transmits payment card information from retail and hospitality to healthcare and fintech. As of March 31, 2025, all 64 new requirements introduced in v4.0 are fully in force and enforceable by qualified security assessors.

Despite this, only 27.9% of organizations are fully compliant at the time of assessment, according to the Verizon Payment Security Report. Forensic investigations consistently show that every payment card breach involved a control explicitly covered by PCI DSS meaning attackers are exploiting known, unaddressed gaps rather than novel techniques. Monthly fines for non-compliance range from $5,000 to $100,000, and the average cost of a payment data breach reached $4.88 million in 2024.

Top 5 Most Common PCI DSS Failures

Based on Verizon forensic investigations, SISA, Hyperproof, and SecurityMetrics assessments. These five gaps appear most frequently across breached organizations globally.

#1

Weak or Missing Access Controls & Authentication

68%
of breached orgs
PCI DSS Req 7 & 8 : Identity & Access Management

Inadequate authentication missing MFA, shared accounts, weak passwords, unchanged default credentials is the single most exploited gap. PCI DSS v4.0 now mandates MFA for all access into the CDE (Req 8.4.2) and a minimum 12-character password (Req 8.3.6).

Notable breaches: Target (41M cards, $18.5M settlement) · Heartland Payment Systems (130M cards) · British Airways (500K customers, $229M fine)

#2

Unpatched Systems & Unmanaged Vulnerabilities

61%
of breached orgs
PCI DSS Req 6 & 11: Vulnerability Management & Security Testing

Failure to apply security patches, run quarterly external scans, and remediate critical CVEs within SLAs. A SecurityMetrics study confirmed every weak point exploited in PCI breaches was explicitly addressed by PCI DSS. Legacy Windows Server and EOL CentOS 7 environments are disproportionately affected.

Notable breaches: Equifax (147M records, $425M settlement -> unpatched Apache Struts) · Adobe (3M card records -> unpatched ColdFusion CVE)

#3

Improper Network Segmentation & Firewall Misconfiguration

57%
of breached orgs
PCI DSS Req 1 & 2: Network Security Controls

Organizations frequently fail to isolate the CDE from other network segments. Misconfigured firewall rules RDP or SSH open to the internet, no default-deny outbound, flat network topology allow attackers to pivot directly into cardholder data systems from a less-secure zone.

Notable breaches: Heartland Payment Systems (175,000 merchants affected) · TJX Companies (94M accounts, $40.9M fine -> flat WiFi network)

#4

Insufficient Logging, Monitoring & Incident Response

54%
of breached orgs
PCI DSS Req 10 & 12: Audit Logs, SIEM & Incident Response

Organizations lacking SIEM deployment, 365-day log retention, File Integrity Monitoring, and a documented daily review process fail to detect breaches in progress. Verizon forensics has never found a breached organization fully PCI compliant at the time of breach.

Notable breaches: Target (active malware alerts for 3 weeks before detection) · Marriott (breach undetected for 4 years -> 500M guest records)

#5

Storing & Mishandling Sensitive Authentication Data (SAD)

49%
of breached orgs
PCI DSS Req 3 & 4: Cardholder Data Protection & Encryption

Many organizations unknowingly store full magnetic stripe data, CVV codes, or PINs post-authorization a direct PCI violation. PAN transmission without TLS and lack of tokenization are frequently cited. Card data is often discovered in unexpected places: logs, emails, and unencrypted databases.

Notable breaches: Wawa (30M cards -> stored full track data) · Sonic Drive-In (5M cards -> POS memory scraping of unencrypted PANs)

Remediation Roadmap

Short-Term (0–30 Days)

  • Enforce MFA for all remote access into the CDE (Req 8.4.3).
  • Patch all critical CVEs across scoped systems.
  • Restrict RDP (3389) to VPN gateway IPs only.
  • Run overdue external vulnerability scan.

Medium-Term (30–90 Days)

  • Extend log retention to 365 days in SIEM.
  • Deploy FIM (OSSEC/Wazuh) on all log directories.
  • Update password policy to 12-character minimum.
  • Eliminate all shared/generic accounts.

Long-Term (90–180 Days)

  • Implement default-deny outbound firewall policy.
  • Complete Targeted Risk Analysis for all controls.
  • Conduct formal annual PCI DSS scope review (SAQ-D).
  • Deliver mandatory security awareness training.
